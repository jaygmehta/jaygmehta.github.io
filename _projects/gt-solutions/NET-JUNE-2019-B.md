Consider the set of matrices
$$G =\left\{\begin{pmatrix}
s & b \\ 0 & 1 \
\end{pmatrix} : b \in \mathbb Z, s \in \{-1,1\}\right\}.$$
Then which of the following is true?

(a) $G$ forms a group under addition<br>
(b) $G$ forms an abelian group under multiplication.<br>
(c) Every element in $G$ is diagonalisable over $\mathbb C$<br>
(d) $G$ is finitely generated abelian group under multiplication<br>

**Solution**

(a) Consider two matrices $A=(a_{ij})$ and $B=(b_{ij})$ in $G$ with $a_{22}=1$ and $b_{22}=1$. Then $a_{22}+b_{22}= 2 \neq 1$. Hence, $A+B \not\in G$.<br>
(b) **Not True**.\\
$\begin{pmatrix}
s_1 & b_1\\ 0 & 1
\end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix}
s_2 & b_2\\ 0 & 1
\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}
s_1 s_2 & s_1b_2+ b_1 \\ 0 & 1
\end{pmatrix} \in G$.\\
Identity matrix is identity of multiplication and $s_2 = s_1$, $b_2 = -s_1 b_1$ gives the inverse matrix of $\begin{pmatrix}
s_1 & b_1\\ 0 & 1
\end{pmatrix}$. Thus, $G$ is a group but not abelian because\\
$\begin{pmatrix}
1 & b_1\\ 0 & 1
\end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix}
-1 & b_2\\ 0 & 1
\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}
-1 & b_2+ b_1\\ 0 & 1
\end{pmatrix} \neq \begin{pmatrix}
-1 & b_2 - b_1\\ 0 & 1
\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}
-1 & b_2\\ 0 & 1
\end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix}
1 & b_1\\ 0 & 1
\end{pmatrix}$

(c) We know that ($s=1,b=1$) the matrix $\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 1 \\ 0 & 1
\end{pmatrix}$ is not diagonalisable (since if it is diagonalisable, then as both eigenvalues are $1$, it must be similar and hence equal to identity matrix). So this option is **not correct**.<br>

(d) Part B, all other options are incorrect. So this option is correct.\\
Note that $G$ is generated by two elements $\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 
\end{pmatrix}$ and $\begin{pmatrix}
-1 & 1 \\ 0 & 1
\end{pmatrix}$.
\end{enumerate}
