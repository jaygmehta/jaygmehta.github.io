Let $p \ge 23$ be a prime number such that the decimal expansion (base $10$) of $\frac{1}{p}$ is periodic with period $p-1$ (that is $\frac{1}{p} = 0.\overline{a_1a_2\cdots a_{p-1}}$ with $a_i\in \{0,1,\ldots,9\}$ for all $i$) and for any $m$, $1 \le m < p-1$, $\frac{1}{p} \neq 0.\overline{a_1a_2\cdots a_{m}}$.

Let $(\mathbb Z/p\mathbb Z)^*$ denote the multiplicative group of integers modulo $p$. Then which of the following is correct?

(a) he order of $10 \in (\mathbb Z/p\mathbb Z)^*$ is a proper divisor of $(p-1)$<br>
(b) The order of $10 \in (\mathbb Z/p\mathbb Z)^*$ is $\frac{(p-1)}{2}$<br>
(c) The element $10 \in (\mathbb Z/p\mathbb Z)^*$ is a generator of the group $(\mathbb Z/ p\mathbb Z)^*$<br>
(d) The group $(\mathbb Z/p\mathbb Z)^*$ is cyclic but not generated by the element $10$<br>

**Solution**

Suppose $\frac{1}{p}$ has $u$ period, i.e. $\frac{1}{p}= 0. a_1a_2\cdots a_u \ a_1a_2\cdots a_u \cdots = 0. \overline{a_1a_2\cdots a_u}$. Then multiplying by $10^u$ on both sides we get
\begin{align*}
&\ 10^u\times \frac{1}{p} = a_1a_2\cdots a_n. \overline{a_1a_2\cdots a_n}\\
\Rightarrow &\ 10^u\times \frac{1}{p} =  {a_1a_2\cdots a_n} \times  0.\overline{a_1a_2\cdots a_n} =  {a_1a_2\cdots a_n} \times \frac{1}{p} \\
\Rightarrow &\ (10^u-1)\times \frac{1}{p} =   {a_1a_2\cdots a_n}\\
\Rightarrow &\ 10^u - 1 =  {a_1a_2\cdots a_n} \times p\\
\Rightarrow &\ 10^u \equiv 1 \pmod p.
\end{align*}
If this period $u$ is the smallest integers such that $10^u \equiv 1 \pmod p$, then it is called the order of $10$ modulo $p$. If it is not the smallest, then a divisor of $u$ can possibly be order of $10$ modulo $p$. By Euler-Fermat's theorem, $10^{p-1} \equiv 1 \pmod p$. Therefore, if $u$ is the order of $10$ modulo $p$, then in general the order $u \mid p-1$.\\
Here, the period of $\frac{1}{p}$ is given to be $p-1$ and not less than that (such primes are called full-period primes). Hence, the (multiplicative) order of $10$ modulo $p$, i.e. in $(\mathbb Z/p\mathbb Z)^*$ is $p-1$.
